Press Kit

A founding chief's retirement, and a museum built by his people. Everything a newsroom needs is on this page — the story, the facts, quotes, photos to download, and how to reach us for interviews.

The story

For 34 years, Chief Harold “Buster” Hatcher led the Waccamaw Indian People — the tribe he founded in 1992 and the first state-recognized tribe in South Carolina. On June 29, 2026, he retired. His people answered his farewell by building him a museum: his story, more than three decades of photographs, the origins of the tribe in the Dimery Settlement, five generations of family history, and his own voice on tape. It is online now at museum.waccamaw.org.

A Vietnam combat veteran and Chief Warrant Officer, Hatcher spent his life asserting and defending Native identity in South Carolina. He won a landmark 1988 lawsuit affirming that identity, led the tribe to state recognition in 2005, and helped change state law so Native chiefs could perform legal marriages. In 2022 the tribe filed a petition for federal recognition, which remains pending. Leadership now passes to Chief Cheryl Cail.

How this museum was built

The Waccamaw Indian People have kept records, photographs, and recordings for decades — scattered across boxes, drives, and memory. To bring them together, the tribe used new AI tools to help sort and connect that corpus: surfacing documents, matching photographs to events, and organizing oral histories that might otherwise have stayed buried. Every entry was reviewed and confirmed by tribal members. The tools did the sifting; the People did the telling — a kind of digital preservation usually reserved for large institutions, here done by a small tribe, for its own history, and given freely to the public.
