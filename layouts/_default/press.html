For the Press · Waccamaw Indian People

Press Kit

Background, facts, quotes, and photos on the Waccamaw Indian People, South Carolina's first state-recognized tribe. The current news is our founding chief's retirement and a new digital museum. You'll also find his civil-rights legacy, our annual Pauwau, and how to reach us for interviews.

The news

For 34 years, Chief Harold “Buster” Hatcher led the Waccamaw Indian People, the tribe he founded in 1992 and the first state-recognized tribe in South Carolina. On June 29, 2026, he retired. His people answered his farewell by building him a museum: his story, more than three decades of photographs, the origins of the tribe in the Dimery Settlement, five generations of family history, and his own voice on tape. It is online now at museum.waccamaw.org.

Leadership now passes to Chief Cheryl Cail, the tribe's incoming chief.

Chief Harold “Buster” Hatcher

Harold “Buster” Hatcher served twenty years with the Army's 82nd Airborne and came home a Purple Heart combat veteran. He spent the rest of his life making South Carolina recognize the Native people it had long refused to count.

In 1992 he founded the Waccamaw Indian People. He took the state to court to affirm Native identity and lobbied every South Carolina governor from Carroll Campbell onward. He chaired a coalition of more than 400 tribes, briefed President Clinton, and won relief in federal law for non-federal Indians. He secured the right for a Native chief to perform a legal marriage in South Carolina, and he persuaded Myrtle Beach to trade Columbus Day for Indigenous Peoples' Day. On February 17, 2005, he led the Waccamaw to become the first state-recognized tribe in South Carolina's history. He retired on June 29, 2026, after 34 years as chief.

Honors: the Jefferson Award for service to the American Indian community, an honorary Doctor of Humane Letters from Coastal Carolina University, and a place in the Library of Congress Veterans History Project.

His story is drawn from his own oral histories and public interviews, among them the Library of Congress Veterans History Project, Coastal Carolina University's Waccamaw Voices, and reporting by WBTW and WPDE.

  • Where: The Dog Bluff community near Aynor, Horry County, S.C., with 300+ years of continuous presence.

  • Recognition: State-recognized since 2005. Federal recognition petition filed 2022, still pending.

The Waccamaw Pauwau

Every year on the first full weekend of November, the tribe hosts the Waccamaw Pauwau & Cultural Arts Festival on its grounds near Aynor. It is a public gathering of dancing, drumming, regalia, cultural arts, food, and vendors, and it has grown every year since the first Pauwau in 1992. The 2026 festival is November 7 and 8, the 34th annual. Admission and parking are free, and camping and RV space are available on site.

How this museum was built

The Waccamaw Indian People have kept records, photographs, and recordings for decades, scattered across boxes, drives, and memory. To bring them together, the tribe used new AI tools to help sort and connect that corpus: surfacing documents, matching photographs to events, and organizing oral histories that might otherwise have stayed buried. Every entry was reviewed and confirmed by tribal members. The tools did the sifting. The People did the telling.

It is also an act of sovereignty. Rather than hand its history to an outside institution, the tribe keeps and stewards its own record: its own data, on its own platform, under its own control. The museum and the systems behind it run on orgware, a member platform built by a tribal member for tribes and nonprofits. More on how it works at orgware.hatcher.ltd.
