CIVIL RIGHTS & RECOGNITION

The modern fight for Waccamaw recognition began with one man who refused to be told that his people did not exist.

Harold "Buster" Hatcher came up in the age of segregated schools, where a Waccamaw child belonged to neither the white world nor the black one. He was beaten and bullied for it, and he left school in the ninth grade. He joined the Army, served in Vietnam, and stayed for twenty years — retiring as a Chief Warrant Officer with three Purple Hearts.

He came home to Conway to build a life. When he went to register a minority-owned business — one he had bought from family and named Diseco, for his sister Dessie — a state office told him, flatly, that there are no Native Americans in South Carolina. That single sentence lit a fire that burned for the rest of his life. In 1992 he founded the Waccamaw Indian People, set out to make the state admit what it had denied, and won along the way the right to run his business as the minority-owned enterprise it had always been.

Through the 1990s he carried the cause to the highest tables in the country, meeting with President Bill Clinton and the Bureau of Indian Affairs and becoming, in effect, the face of the Native American cause in South Carolina.

"Finally, we can be something more than Other." — Chief Harold "Buster" Hatcher

For generations, a Waccamaw filling out an official form found only one box that fit: Other. Around the turn of the millennium — as the U.S. Census for the first time let people claim more than one race, and as the tribe pressed its case for recognition — that indignity began to lift. To be counted, by name, as Native American was its own quiet victory, and the Chief's joke about it has been repeated ever since.

  1. 1992
    A people, formally organized. Chief Hatcher founded the Waccamaw Indian People — and won the standing to run his business as the minority-owned enterprise it had always been.
  2. 1994
    A seat at the national table. Working with President Clinton and the White House, Chief Hatcher secured concessions for American Indians across the country.
  3. c. 2000
    Counted, not lumped in. As the census first allowed more than one race and the tribe pressed for recognition, Native South Carolinians could at last be recorded as who they are — "something more than Other."
  4. 2005
    First in South Carolina. On February 17, 2005, the Waccamaw Indian People became the first tribe in the state's history to receive state recognition.
  5. Ongoing
    Bringing the ancestors home. The tribe continues to lead efforts to repatriate more than 600 sets of ancestral remains held in state museums, so they can rest with the dignity they are owed.
