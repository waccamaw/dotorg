CIVIL RIGHTS & RECOGNITION

The Waccamaw have won real ground — not handed to them, but earned through decades of persistence by leaders who refused to accept that their people did not count.

  1. 1994
    A seat at the national table. Chief Harold "Buster" Hatcher worked with President Bill Clinton and the White House to win concessions for American Indians across the country, bringing a more level playing field for Native people everywhere.
  2. Records
    The right to be counted as who they are. The Waccamaw became the first tribe in South Carolina to gain official recognition from the S.C. Office of Vital Statistics — so a Waccamaw could be recorded, on paper, as Native American instead of mislabeled as something else.
  3. 2003
    Equal protection under state law. In July 2003, South Carolina enacted a law, signed by Governor Sanford, extending to the state's American Indians protections that other citizens had held for decades.
  4. 2005
    First in South Carolina. On February 17, 2005, the Waccamaw Indian People became the first tribe in the state's history to receive state recognition.
  5. Ongoing
    Bringing the ancestors home. The tribe continues to lead efforts to repatriate more than 600 sets of ancestral remains held in state museums, so they can rest with the dignity they are owed.
