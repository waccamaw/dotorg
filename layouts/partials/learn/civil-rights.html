A Civil Rights Leader

Chief Harold "Buster" Hatcher

He spent his life insisting that Native South Carolinians be seen, counted, and treated as equal citizens — and built a recognized tribe out of a state's refusal to admit that his people existed.

"We don't ask for anything better, but we think we should at least get the same."

Harold "Buster" Hatcher grew up straddling two worlds in the age of segregation — a Waccamaw child who belonged to neither. There were black water fountains and white water fountains and black entrances to the restaurants, he remembered, but there were no Indian entrances. It's either black or white. He left school in the ninth grade, joined the Army's 82nd Airborne, and served twenty years — coming home a Purple Heart combat veteran.

Back in South Carolina, he set out to run a minority-owned business — one he had bought from family and named Diseco, for his sister Dessie — and a state office told him, flatly, that there are no Native Americans in South Carolina. That refusal lit a fight that lasted the rest of his life. In 1992 he founded the Waccamaw Indian People and set out to make the state admit what it had denied.

From there his reach only grew. He chaired the National Coalition for Indian Equality — more than four hundred Native groups and tribes across the country. In 1994 he briefed President Bill Clinton on the plight of non-federal Indians and won real relief in federal law. He worked with every South Carolina governor from Carroll Campbell onward, helped found and chaired the state's Indian Affairs Commission, pushed through the 2008 law that let a Native chief perform a legal marriage in South Carolina, and persuaded Myrtle Beach to trade Columbus Day for Indigenous Peoples' Day. In 2005, he led the Waccamaw to become the first state-recognized tribe in South Carolina history.

"Finally, we can be something more than Other." — Chief Harold "Buster" Hatcher

For generations, a Waccamaw filling out an official form found only one box that fit: Other. Around the turn of the millennium — as the U.S. Census for the first time let people claim more than one race, and as the tribe pressed its case for recognition — that indignity began to lift. To be counted, by name, as Native American was its own quiet victory, and the Chief's line about it has been repeated ever since.

  1. 1992
    A people, formally organized. Chief Hatcher founded the Waccamaw Indian People — and won the standing to run his business as the minority-owned enterprise it had always been.
  2. 1994
    A seat at the national table. Chairing a coalition of 400+ tribes, Chief Hatcher briefed President Clinton and won relief in federal law for non-federal Indians.
  3. c. 2000
    Counted, not lumped in. As the census first allowed more than one race and the tribe pressed for recognition, Native South Carolinians could at last be recorded as who they are — "something more than Other."
  4. 2005
    First in South Carolina. On February 17, 2005, the Waccamaw Indian People became the first tribe in the state's history to receive state recognition.
  5. Ongoing
    Bringing the ancestors home. The tribe continues to lead efforts to repatriate more than 600 sets of ancestral remains held in state museums, so they can rest with the dignity they are owed.

His story is drawn from Chief Hatcher's own oral histories and public interviews — among them the Library of Congress Veterans History Project, Coastal Carolina University's Waccamaw Voices, and reporting by WBTW and WPDE.
